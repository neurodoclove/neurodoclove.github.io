<h2 id="bio">What drives us?</h2>

<div style="display: flex; align-items: flex-start; gap: 20px; margin-bottom: 2em;">
    
    <div style="flex: 1;">
        <p>
            When we reach for food, scroll through a feed, work toward a goal, process pain, or avoid a threat, our brain's reinforcement circuits are quietly calculating and helping us prioritize the actions that are most likely to ensure our survival or provide relief. Whether seeking a reward or escaping a threat, our internal valuation systems weigh the effort required against the potential outcome to dictate our next move. These circuits evolved to compel us to act and to close the gap between our current state and, at least potentially, a better state.
        </p>     
        <p>
            When the neural mechanisms supporting these abilities fail to operate properly, reactions to natural rewards like food, sex, and social interaction can be compromised. In depression, this can manifest as a loss of motivation to pursue everyday pleasures. In chronic pain, the same circuitry becomes fixated on the need to escape suffering. In addiction, drugs and alcohol gradually rewire these pathways until these substances become the only reward the brain deems worth pursuing. With this shared neural machinery, it's no surprise that depression, chronic pain, and addiction so often travel together in the same person.
        </p>
        <p>
            Our research tries to understand how activity within these circuits can go awry and how that knowledge might eventually point toward better ways to treat all three.
        </p>
    </div>

<h2 id="bio">Our work</h2>

<p> Our lab uses a technique called deep phenotyping to help us understand how psychiatric illnesses develop, identify how recovery begins, and how that recovery might be encouraged. While a phenotype is a set of observable characteristics, 'deep' phenotyping involves taking a detailed look at traits that are not easily seen. For this, we use technologies like neuroimaging, genetics, and ambient sensing to capture as much information as we can about an individual and develop a more complete picture of mental health. Our goal is to uncover the mechanisms behind these diseases so we can detect problems early and develop treatments that are as personalized and intricate as the circuits they seek to restore.</p>
